# SewerVerdict Success Council

This file is the hard-edged success doctrine for SewerVerdict.
It exists to reduce the main ways this project could fail in the first 6 months.

Use it when deciding:

- what to build next
- what to cut
- what to polish
- what to ignore

Goal:

- create the highest-probability path to **KRW 1,000,000 monthly revenue by 2026-09-16**

This document does not promise success.
It does remove a lot of avoidable failure.

## Council

### 1. The Search Wedge Architect

Position:

- We do not need to become the biggest sewer content site.
- We need to become the most useful site at the exact decision moment.

Hard argument:

- A new domain is unlikely to beat giant publishers by breadth.
- It can beat them by sharper intent match:
  - buyer under contract
  - hidden sewer risk before closing
  - cast iron and orangeburg pain
  - red-flag interpretation
  - buyer versus seller leverage

Demand:

- protect the buyer-intent wedge
- protect the defect-interpretation wedge
- do not drift into generic plumbing coverage

Decision:

- the win is not "more pages"
- the win is "better pages for the exact anxious decision"

### 2. The Conversion Economist

Position:

- This business is won by qualified action, not pageviews alone.

Hard argument:

- The traffic can stay modest if the intent is sharp enough.
- Therefore the real leverage is:
  - trust on landing
  - completion of the estimator
  - calm next-step framing
  - low-friction lead capture
  - partner-usable inspection-first demand

Demand:

- every important page must route toward action
- every CTA must match evidence state
- every form must feel lighter than the value of the answer

Decision:

- optimize for qualified leads per 1,000 sessions, not vanity traffic

### 3. The Skeptical Buyer Psychologist

Position:

- Buyers do not reward the loudest site.
- They reward the site that lowers anxiety without sounding weak.

Hard argument:

- The user does not want a sewer lecture.
- The user wants permission to decide:
  - scope now
  - quote now
  - negotiate now
  - wait

Demand:

- no contractor tone
- no "you must replace it now" shortcuts
- keep decision language ahead of repair language

Decision:

- if the page cannot be safely sent to an agent or seller, it is not good enough

### 4. The Trust Editor

Position:

- Trust cues must appear before the user asks whether the site is credible.

Hard argument:

- This topic touches money, transactions, liability, and insurance confusion.
- If trust signals are buried, users downgrade the site immediately.

Demand:

- visible review cues near the top
- methodology links on important surfaces
- source-backed language on money and responsibility pages
- uncertainty phrasing that keeps the page useful

Decision:

- trust is not a footer feature
- trust is part of the conversion path

### 5. The Distribution Realist

Position:

- The first compounding growth may come from shared usefulness, not just rankings.

Hard argument:

- Pages that settle arguments travel.
- Pages that only summarize broad topics do not.

Most shareable assets:

- sewer scope before buying
- sewer scope red flags
- buyer or seller responsibility
- cast iron and orangeburg guides
- estimator decision memo

Demand:

- make pages share-safe
- make result summaries copy-friendly
- make titles obvious about the decision being solved

Decision:

- build pages that can survive outside Google

### 6. The Mobile Product Perfectionist

Position:

- If it feels cheap on a small phone, the business loses before copy can help.

Hard argument:

- Many of these sessions happen in transit, in the field, or inside text threads.
- Premium trust is mostly perceived in the first 15 seconds on mobile.

Demand:

- no unstable header behavior
- no intimidating forms
- no hidden trust cues on mobile
- no awkward long-form wall before value is clear

Decision:

- iPhone SE quality is not polish
- it is go-to-market quality

### 7. The Partner and Routing Operator

Position:

- Inspection-first routing is a business advantage, not just nicer wording.

Hard argument:

- Many users do not need a replacement quote yet.
- Routing them to quotes too early weakens trust and can hurt partner value.

Demand:

- scope-first when evidence is weak
- quote-first when evidence is serious or confirmed
- keep routing logic explainable

Decision:

- the right lead is worth much more than the wrong lead

## Consensus

The council agrees on seven truths:

1. SewerVerdict wins as a decision utility, not as a broad publisher.
2. Buyer-intent and defect-intent are the real moat.
3. Trust must appear on the first screen, especially on mobile.
4. Inspection-first routing is a strategic advantage, not a compromise.
5. Shareability is a growth feature.
6. Lead friction must stay lower than the user's anxiety threshold.
7. More content only helps if it sharpens intent capture or trust.

## Success Doctrine

For the next 6 months, every meaningful change should help at least one of these:

1. stronger rankings on buyer-intent and defect-intent pages
2. better first-screen trust
3. better estimator completion
4. better shareability of results and pages
5. better qualified lead submission
6. better partner-friendly inspection-first routing

If a change does not improve one of the six, it is probably optional.

## Failure Modes To Avoid

The council believes the project fails if it does one of these:

1. becomes a generic plumbing content site
2. becomes a nice-looking site with weak routing and weak forms
3. becomes a cost-blog clone without stronger decision value
4. becomes over-designed and less trustworthy
5. expands page count faster than it expands trust and usefulness
6. captures low-quality leads that do not map to real commercial demand

## The Only Rational Build Order

1. keep the top decision pages sharper than broader competitors
2. keep trust cues high and visible
3. keep estimator and result surfaces strong enough to be shared
4. keep lead forms light enough to finish on mobile
5. keep scope-first and quote-first routing honest
6. deploy, measure, and tighten winners instead of widening too early

## Hard Filters

Before shipping a new page or feature, ask:

1. does this help a buyer, seller, or owner make a real next-step decision?
2. would this page still be useful if it were shared in a text thread?
3. does this reduce anxiety without reducing honesty?
4. does this improve the revenue spine directly or indirectly?
5. would I still build this if I only had 6 months to prove the business?

If the answer is mostly no, cut it.

## Final Council Call

SewerVerdict does not need to look unbeatable.
It needs to become hard to dismiss.

That means:

- more trusted than spammy competitors
- more decisive than generic publishers
- more useful than noisy contractor pages
- more shareable than blog content
- more honest than lead traps

If that standard is met consistently, the project earns its path.
