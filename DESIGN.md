# SewerVerdict Design Anchor

This file fixes the visual and UX direction for SewerVerdict.
Use it with `AGENTS.md`.

The goal is not generic "nice design."
The goal is **trust that converts** for a stressed homeowner decision.

Modernity is part of trust for this product.
The site should not look old, bloggy, or templated.
It should look current, deliberate, and premium without becoming flashy.

Design is not just brand expression here.
Design quality must help the business reach the revenue target by increasing trust, comprehension, and conversion quality.

## Chosen Direction

The primary design direction for SewerVerdict is:

**home due-diligence desk**

This means the product should feel like a blend of:

- a modern inspection summary
- a homeowner decision memo
- a civic or insurance explainer

This is the best-fit direction for the topic because:

- the user is often under stress and needs calm authority
- the decision is expensive, uncertain, and easy to oversell
- trust matters more than visual excitement
- results need to be shareable with agents, sellers, inspectors, and contractors

If a future design choice conflicts with this direction, choose the option that feels more like a **due-diligence tool** and less like a **lead-gen landing page**.

The visual execution should still feel clearly **modern**:

- modern layout discipline
- modern spacing and typography
- modern card and form treatment
- modern interaction polish

But not:

- SaaS-dashboard coded
- startup-hype coded
- emergency-marketplace coded

## Design Thesis

SewerVerdict should feel like:

- a calm homeowner decision utility
- a trustworthy inspection and cost explainer
- a serious editorial product with operational clarity

It should **not** feel like:

- a local plumber ad
- an emergency lead-trap
- a generic SaaS dashboard
- a content-farm blog

Working design phrase:

**calm document-grade decision UX**

Supporting phrase:

**modern premium utility**

That means the UI should feel closer to:

- a good inspection summary
- a mortgage or insurance explainer
- a modern civic-service tool

and farther from:

- contractor landing pages
- startup-neon dashboards
- affiliate listicle sites

## Persona Council

Design direction was chosen by balancing these personas.

### 1. Buyer under contract

What this person needs from the design:

- immediate clarity
- emotional de-escalation
- confidence that the site is not trying to scare them into buying
- a path from uncertainty -> next step -> negotiation readiness

What breaks trust:

- aggressive red alerts everywhere
- cheesy contractor imagery
- oversimplified "you must replace it now" copy
- cluttered quote-form-first landing pages

### 2. Seller pre-listing

What this person needs:

- neutral framing
- no blame-heavy language
- help preparing before the issue becomes a negotiation problem

What breaks trust:

- accusation-style copy
- buyer-only framing
- panic visuals

### 3. Existing owner with symptoms or a known finding

What this person needs:

- seriousness without panic
- a believable sense of triage
- clear differences between "inspect first" and "quote now"

What breaks trust:

- vague blog styling
- over-designed, over-animated interfaces
- medical-emergency tone

### 4. Agent / inspector / share recipient

This is the indirect persona who sees copied summaries, shared pages, or printed notes.

What this person needs:

- scannable structure
- shareable logic
- concise evidence of methodology
- pages that look citeable, not promotional

What breaks trust:

- ad-heavy layouts
- inflated marketing claims
- no visible methodology or review cues

## Final Direction

The chosen direction is:

**editorial utility + inspection-report discipline + restrained homeowner warmth**

In practice:

- editorial enough to feel researched
- operational enough to drive action
- warm enough to feel human
- restrained enough to preserve trust

### Directions explicitly rejected

Do not drift into these modes:

1. contractor ad aesthetic
2. fintech dashboard aesthetic
3. urgent repair marketplace aesthetic
4. generic affiliate blog aesthetic

If unsure, ask:

- does this look like a serious decision tool?
- does this feel neutral enough to be shared in a transaction?
- would a buyer trust this page enough to send it to an agent?

## Visual Character

### Mood

- calm
- grounded
- competent
- high-trust
- low-drama
- current
- premium

### Visual references to emulate at a pattern level

- modern document tools
- premium insurance explainer pages
- mortgage or home-buying decision tools
- inspection-report summaries

Do not mimic brand-identifying visuals.
Copy structural ideas, information density, block rhythm, and trust cues only.

### Anti-direction

Avoid:

- plumber trucks
- workers-with-wrenches hero photos
- sewage closeups
- glossy stock-family imagery
- bright emergency red everywhere
- black/charcoal "luxury" dark themes
- purple startup defaults

## Color System

Use a restrained palette that feels like paper, ink, metal, and field notes.

Suggested tokens:

- `--bg`: `#F5F1E8`
- `--surface`: `#FFFDF8`
- `--surface-alt`: `#ECE6DA`
- `--ink`: `#1F2A30`
- `--muted`: `#5F6B72`
- `--line`: `#D7D0C4`
- `--brand`: `#1E5563`
- `--brand-strong`: `#133B44`
- `--accent`: `#8A6A3A`
- `--risk-low`: `#56705C`
- `--risk-medium`: `#8B6B3F`
- `--risk-high`: `#8A4A3F`

Usage rules:

- background should be warm and quiet, not pure white
- most text should be dark ink, not full black
- brand color should be used for trust and action, not hype
- high-risk color should appear as a signal, not as the page atmosphere

## Typography

The typography should make the product feel both researched and usable.

Recommended pair:

- headings: `Newsreader`
- body / UI: `Public Sans`

Fallback pair:

- headings: `Georgia`
- body / UI: system sans

Rules:

- headings should feel editorial, not tech-startup
- body text should feel civic, plain, and highly legible
- use strong type hierarchy before adding more visual decoration
- cost ranges, labels, and checklists should rely on spacing and weight, not flashy color
- typography should feel contemporary and intentional, never default-template

## Modernity Standard

SewerVerdict should feel like a product built now, not five years ago.

Use modern patterns such as:

- strong section spacing
- crisp grids
- clear content density control
- polished card composition
- segmented choice controls
- structured step flows
- clean sticky utility elements where helpful

Do not use dated patterns such as:

- crowded blog sidebars
- tiny link-heavy navs
- generic hero + stock photo + form stacks
- bevel-heavy UI
- old bootstrap-looking tables and forms
- oversized glossy buttons

## Layout System

### Structure

- mobile-first
- generous vertical rhythm
- strong max-width control
- simple grid
- clear section boundaries

Suggested widths:

- reading sections: `68ch` to `76ch`
- utility sections and cards: `1120px` max container

### Rhythm

- hero -> trust strip -> persona/issue lanes -> explainer blocks -> CTA
- every important page should show the "answer first"
- long pages should alternate between narrative sections and utility blocks

### Shape language

- medium radius, not bubble-round
- lightly layered cards
- visible borders
- restrained shadows
- edges should feel crisp and current, not soft and playful

## Core UX Rules

### 1. The site should guide, not perform

Do not make the UI feel like it is showing off.
The user should feel:

- "this is organized"
- "this understands my situation"
- "I know what to do next"
- "this looks professionally designed"

### 2. Trust cues must be visible early

Important pages should expose near the top:

- reviewed / methodology cues
- range-based pricing language
- what the page can and cannot know

### 3. CTA pressure must be controlled

Primary actions should be clear, but the page should never feel like a lead trap.

The goal is high-trust conversion, not soft engagement.
Design should help more qualified users reach the estimator or lead form without making the product feel pushy.

Preferred CTA framing:

- `Start the estimator`
- `Find sewer camera inspection options`
- `Get sewer repair or replacement quotes`
- `Copy summary`

Avoid:

- `Get help now`
- `Claim your deal`
- `Instant sewer fix`
- `Emergency service`

### 4. Pages must answer before they ask

Never put a lead form before the page has earned trust.
On commercial pages:

- quick answer first
- explanation second
- CTA third

### 5. Results should feel like a decision memo

Estimator results should look like:

- a short field summary
- a risk call
- next-step instructions
- cost direction
- uncertainties
- a clean block to share

Not like:

- a casino dashboard
- a fake AI diagnosis screen

## Homepage Direction

The homepage should feel like a cross between:

- a risk explainer
- a utility launcher
- a high-trust commercial content hub

Homepage order:

1. clear hero
2. trust strip
3. persona entry
4. main issue lanes
5. "how it works"
6. featured money pages
7. methodology/disclaimer callout
8. FAQ

Hero direction:

- left side: direct headline and route choice
- right side: calm visual artifact, not a form wall

Good visual artifact ideas:

- abstract parcel / pipe path diagram
- annotated house-to-street cross-section
- layered decision cards

The hero should feel like the front page of a decision brief, not a campaign landing page.
It should also feel premium enough that the design itself raises confidence.

## Estimator Direction

The estimator should feel fast, guided, and low-friction.

Recommended pattern:

- multi-step card flow
- strong step title
- 3-6 choice buttons per step
- sticky progress cue
- optional side panel or footer note for "why this matters"

Rules:

- one decision at a time
- no dense giant form
- make old-house / urgency / issue-state logic feel understandable
- on mobile, choices should feel thumb-friendly and not overpacked
- controls should feel product-grade, not form-builder-grade

## Result Page Direction

The result page is the product trust peak.

The top block should include:

- risk label
- short interpretation
- likely next step
- primary CTA
- secondary CTA

Below that:

- what this likely means
- cost band cards
- biggest uncertainty drivers
- three questions to ask next
- copyable summary
- methodology/disclaimer box

The result should feel saved, organized, and shareable.

The visual model is a short, well-designed field memo.

## Content Page Direction

Every indexable page should blend:

- answer-first utility
- decision support
- trust scaffolding
- modest commercial routing

Preferred block rhythm:

1. quick answer
2. seriousness
3. next step
4. cost direction or scenario table
5. what changes the answer
6. CTA block
7. methodology/source box
8. FAQ / related pages

## Trust Components

These are not decorative. They are product-critical.

### Trust strip

Short, high-signal items only:

- ranges, not fake certainty
- reviewed pages
- methodology-backed estimates

### Methodology box

Should look like a concise note from a careful reviewer, not a legal wall.

### Source transparency box

Should look like a compact evidence panel:

- source class
- last verified date
- uncertainty note

### Reviewer metadata

Should be quiet but visible.
Not oversized author-bio marketing.

## Component Style

### Cards

- paper-like surfaces
- visible border
- restrained elevation
- strong heading
- modern spacing and alignment discipline

### Tables

- used heavily for comparison pages
- sticky headers on mobile only if clean
- never make them feel spreadsheet-hostile

### FAQ

- accordion is acceptable
- default styling should feel like a documentation system, not a marketing trap

### Forms

- short
- clean labels
- no dark-pattern copy
- consent copy in plain English
- inputs and buttons should look premium and current, not commodity templates

## Imagery and Illustration

Preferred:

- line drawings
- map / parcel / pipe-path abstractions
- house cross-sections
- simple document-style diagrams

Acceptable:

- subtle textures or patterns
- carefully chosen architectural or street detail photography

Avoid:

- sewage imagery
- plumber portraits
- smiling call-center shots
- unrealistic staged families

## Motion

Use minimal motion.

Allowed:

- light fade/slide on section reveal
- hover emphasis on cards
- step transitions in estimator
- subtle polish that makes the interface feel expensive and considered

Avoid:

- bouncy micro-interactions
- parallax
- animated counters
- flashy loading theatrics

## Kit and Library Guidance

If a UI kit speeds implementation, use it aggressively at the **pattern** level.

Preferred approach for this repo:

- borrow layout and component patterns from established kits
- implement them in server-rendered templates and local CSS
- do not import a heavy React-only dependency just for visuals
- prefer kits that feel premium, neutral, and content-capable

Good kit categories to borrow from:

- neutral app/document kits
- table / form / accordion / breadcrumb kits
- content-heavy marketing blocks with restrained styling
- modern editorial or fintech-adjacent kits without hype aesthetics

Pattern borrowing is encouraged for:

- forms
- tables
- accordions
- breadcrumbs
- tabs if needed
- cards
- content section rhythm

Do not copy:

- branding
- illustrations
- signature visual gimmicks
- exact copy

## Design Copy Rules

Design-copy is allowed as a practical tool.
But the target to copy is **structure**, not brand identity.

Allowed to copy:

- section order
- spacing rhythm
- trust-cue placement
- comparison-table layout
- estimator step patterns
- result-summary structure

Not allowed to copy:

- exact wording
- distinctive visual signatures
- logos
- icon sets tied to another brand

## Decision Keywords

When making UI decisions, bias toward:

- calm
- legible
- citeable
- shareable
- non-pushy
- operationally clear
- modern
- premium

Avoid choices that feel:

- loud
- gimmicky
- salesy
- startup-generic
- plumber-adjacent

## Short Build Rule

If there is a choice between:

- a visually louder design that looks more "designed"
- a calmer design that increases trust and comprehension

choose the calmer design.

If there is a choice between:

- a dated but safe component
- a more modern component that preserves clarity

choose the more modern component.

If there is a choice between:

- a custom one-off visual flourish
- a reusable content or trust component

choose the reusable component.

## Final Standard

SewerVerdict should look like the most trustworthy page a stressed homeowner can land on when trying to understand a buried sewer-line problem.

It should make users think:

- "this looks serious"
- "this looks fair"
- "this is helping me decide"
- "this feels modern enough to trust"
- "I would actually submit my info here if I needed help"

not:

- "this is trying to sell me before helping me"
