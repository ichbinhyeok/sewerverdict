# SewerVerdict Pre-Deploy Persona Council

This file captures the smartest pre-deploy arguments for SewerVerdict.
Use it before launch-day polishing.

Purpose:

- sharpen what wins trust
- avoid building the wrong kind of "complete"
- decide what matters before deploy tomorrow

Grounded against current search and category patterns:

- broad national cost pages are already crowded
- buyer-intent sewer scope framing is real and current
- shareable, transaction-safe trust matters as much as raw keyword coverage

## Council

### 1. The SEO Strategist

Position:

- We should not try to beat generic cost sites by being broader.
- We should beat them by being more useful at the decision moment.

Hard argument:

- Cost SERPs are already occupied by broad home-improvement publishers.
- A new domain usually loses if it publishes the same "average cost plus drivers" page in weaker brand form.
- SewerVerdict should win on information gain:
  - better next-step framing
  - clearer risk ranking
  - stronger issue comparison
  - more transaction-specific interpretation

Demand:

- Keep money pages commercial.
- But make every money page answer:
  - what this means
  - what usually comes next
  - what changes the decision

Decision:

- Do not become a thinner Forbes or This Old House.
- Become a better decision page.

### 2. The Skeptical Buyer Under Contract

Position:

- I do not care about plumbing trivia.
- I care whether I should scope now, negotiate now, or stop worrying.

Hard argument:

- The site earns trust when it lowers emotional noise.
- Buyer pages should feel like purchase-risk guidance, not repair marketing.
- Pages that sound like contractor pages lose the buyer immediately.

Demand:

- Strong buyer framing at the top of buyer pages.
- More "what this means for the deal" language.
- More credit-versus-repair-versus-more-inspection framing.

Decision:

- SewerVerdict must feel safe to send to an agent or seller.

### 3. The Share Recipient

This is the agent, inspector, seller, or contractor who receives a copied result or a shared URL.

Position:

- If the page is not citeable, it will not travel.

Hard argument:

- Shared pages need a neutral tone, visible review cues, and fast scannability.
- The product has to look like a document someone can act on.
- Methodology, reviewer role, summary blocks, and tables are not decoration; they are share infrastructure.

Demand:

- More pages should feel memo-like.
- Important pages should be quoteable in one glance.
- Results and decision pages should remain compact and copyable.

Decision:

- Pages should optimize for "can this be shared in a deal thread?" not just "does it rank?"

### 4. The Conversion Operator

Position:

- Trust is the real conversion engine here, not urgency tricks.

Hard argument:

- The right CTA depends on evidence state.
- Unconfirmed issues should route to inspection-first.
- Confirmed or severe issues should route to quotes.
- Lead forms must feel like routing and triage, not like a marketplace trap.

Demand:

- Keep primary CTA logic aligned with issue certainty.
- Keep lead forms short.
- Keep success states calm and credible.

Decision:

- Conversion improves when the site sounds selective and honest.

### 5. The Mobile Product Designer

Position:

- If mobile feels cramped, cheap, or unstable, trust disappears before the content has a chance.

Hard argument:

- This topic will be consumed in the field, in group chats, and during stressed research sessions.
- Mobile quality is not polish; it is core trust.
- Overflow, awkward tables, and long unbroken forms feel low-grade immediately.

Demand:

- No page-level horizontal scroll.
- Tables must degrade safely on small screens.
- CTA spacing must stay calm and premium on iPhone SE width.

Decision:

- The site should feel composed on the smallest realistic screen, not only on desktop.

### 6. The Distribution Realist

Position:

- The first real traction may come from shareability and niche trust, not from volume SEO alone.

Hard argument:

- Pages should work when dropped into Reddit, a buyer text thread, or an agent conversation.
- The strongest early pages are the ones people can use to settle a real argument.
- This favors:
  - sewer scope before buying
  - sewer scope red flags
  - buyer versus seller responsibility
  - cast iron and orangeburg pages
  - estimator summaries

Demand:

- Keep pages practical and compact enough to share.
- Keep titles and page openings obvious about the decision at stake.

Decision:

- Build pages that travel.

## Consensus

The council agrees on five things:

1. SewerVerdict should win as a decision utility, not as a generic home-improvement publisher.
2. Buyer-intent and defect-interpretation pages are more strategically valuable than broad generic plumbing expansion.
3. Trust cues are product features, not support material.
4. Mobile polish is part of conversion, not a later phase.
5. The best early pages are the ones a buyer or owner can immediately share with another person.

## Pre-Deploy Priorities

These are the highest-leverage improvements before deployment:

1. tighten home and key page openings so the decision being solved is obvious in the first screen
2. keep money pages in decision-first language, not generic cost-blog language
3. preserve inspection-first CTA logic when certainty is low
4. make mobile surfaces feel stable, premium, and non-spammy
5. improve shareability of high-value pages and estimator output

## Anti-Drift Rules

Do not drift into:

- broad plumbing encyclopedia behavior
- city-swap page expansion before the first three anchors prove useful
- overdesigned SaaS behavior that weakens trust
- contractor-ad landing-page tone
- cost-page bloat that adds words without helping the decision

## Final Call

Before deploy, the right question is not:

- "is the site complete?"

The right question is:

- "does this feel trustworthy enough, useful enough, and sharp enough to earn one real lead from one real anxious homeowner?"

If the answer becomes yes repeatedly, the site has a path.
